PARAGRAPH

All that glitters are not gold is an aphorism stating that not everything that looks precious or true turns out to be so. The phrase is a translation of a Latin proverb, Non omne quod nitet aurum est, which appears in the 12th-century Latin poem Speculum Stultorum by the twelfth-century poet Walter Map. The original poem is a satire on the follies of monastic life. The phrase is often misquoted as "All that glisters is not gold", as in Shakespeare's The Merchant of Venice (Act II, Scene 7), written in 1596. The modern English version of the proverb first appeared in print in 1638 in James Howell's collection of proverbs, Proverbs in English, Italian, French and Spanish.
